\section{Problem Statement}

Managing returnable transport items (RTIs) in a manufacturing supply chain is,
at its core, a closed-loop logistics problem. Raw materials, parts and finished goods flow in one direction, 
from suppliers to customers, but the RTIs that carry them must flow
back. The research question this paper addresses is the effectiveness of a collaborative return strategy of RTIs in a given  SC, in which empty RTIs are rerouted and consolidated along the network, analysing economic and environmental costs. To answer this, we propose an optimisation model that determines the optimal return routes for empty RTIs,
the shipment frequency on each route, and the minimum RTI pool  to prevent production disruptions thereby defining the service network desing for RTIs.

% ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
We model the SC as a directed graph $G = (V, E)$, where each node $i \in V$
represents a manufacturing site and each arc $(i,j) \in E$ represents a
potential transport route, both for emtpy and full RTIs. Figure \ref{fig:SC} illustrates a SC network,
where nodes are manufacturing sites and the black arcs represent the daily average flows of full
RTIs driven by production demand. Each product family requires a specific RTI
type, and the number of RTIs needed per shipment depends on how many raw materials, parts or finished goods fit
in a single RTI, as shown in Figure~\ref{fig:Product-RTI}.

An RTI exists in one of two states: \textit{full}, when loaded with raw materials, parts and finished goods, or \textit{empty}, once its contents have been consumed at the
destination site. Some RTIs can be folded, reducing their volume to decrease transport costs, as shown in Figure~\ref{fig:stacked-rti}.
The simplest RTI return strategy is a one-to-one relationship, i.e., 
for every shipment of full RTIs a supplier sends to a customer, the customer
must return the empty RTIs along the same route, as depicted in
Figure \ref{fig:1to1RTI}. This strategy is operationally straightforward,
since each pair of nodes only coordinates with each other. However, it
is rarely cost-optimal in large SCs, as it ignores consolidation
opportunities along the SC and tends to result in both excessive empty RTIs transport costs and a larger RTI pool than strictly necessary, which implies higher RTI investment and inventory costs.

When a central decision-maker, such as a dominant tier-1 manufacturer, an original equipment manufacturer (OEM) that owns the RTI pool, or a SC platform acting as manufacturing as a service (MaaS) {\color{red} Add reference}, has visibility over the entire network, a collaborative RTI return strategy becomes possible. Rather than using a one-to-one RTI return strategy, the model optimises empty RTI routes, reducing the total distance an empty RTI journeys and consolidating routes. This simultaneously diminishes RTI return costs and the overall RTI pool size. Figure~\ref{fig:sc1to1} shows the one-to-one baseline, while Figure \ref{fig:scshared} illustrates how an optimised collaborative strategy reduces RTI return transport costs on the same network. Determining the service network design is the primary objective of this research.

On certain routes, full RTIs are accompanied by protective accessories,
such as nets or lids, that secure parts during transit. These
\textit{inlays} are themselves reusable and must be returned to the origin
site after unloading. Here the FTL and LTL, also known as groupage, transport modes are addressed. 

The model takes as input the average daily flows of full RTIs between nodes, derived from production plans over a decision horizon chosen by the decision-maker — typically a month, quarter, or year. These averages are assumed to be representative of the period, meaning that demand is sufficiently stable across days for the average to serve as a reliable planning input, without extreme imbalances that would render it misleading. Under this assumption, the model determines a static service network design:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item  Routes for empty RTI and inlay return flows.
    \item The shipment frequency per route, expressed as a
          \textit{queue time} (Q-day), i.e.\ the number of days between
          consecutive dispatches.
    \item The transport mode per route, i.e. FTL or LTL.
    \item The total number of RTIs needed.
\end{enumerate}

Operational-level planning, such as the scheduling of individual shipments or the production decisions of each plant, is outside the scope of this problem.


The problem consists of finding the tradeoff among daily RTI transport cost, which include
transport costs for both full and empty RTIs;  the inventory holding cost of
the RTI pool; and CO$_2$ emissions from RTI transport.






% ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
The problem encompasses a strategic-tactical decision level, using as input data the daily averages of RTI demand flows. In practice, the daily avarage flow of full RTIs between nodes is governed by
medium-to-long-term production contracts and procurement agreements, which
change infrequently. The Q-days and transport modes are likewise set
with a medium-to-long-term frequency rather than
renegotiated day by day or week by week. By optimizing strategic-tactical decisions, instead of multi-horizon decions, the model is computationally
tractable even for large industrial networks, where a fully operational
day-by-day optimisation would be intractable. The strategic-tactical
model thus provides a rigorous, scalable upper bound on the achievable savings
from collaborative RTI management, which can subsequently serve as a benchmark
and input for operational-level planning.
